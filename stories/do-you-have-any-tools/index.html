Do You Have Any Tools?

A story about capability — and the moment you realise you’ve been playing on hard mode.

One of the first major purchases I made when I started working was a car.

It took a while to save for it, but once I crossed the line from looking to buying, it felt like a genuine milestone. Independence. Movement. Possibility.

Naturally, once I had the car, I wanted to accessorise.

And I needed tools.

So my second major purchase was a full set of tools. With the help of an older salesman — clearly someone who knew his way around a workshop — I explained what I wanted to do. He nodded, selected the basics, and added a few extras “just in case.”

I was just as proud of my shiny new tools as I was of the car itself.

My friends, however, were quick to point out the obvious.

“Do you actually know what to do with them?”

Uh… no.

Undeterred, I set the car up properly. Full tool kit in the boot at all times. That was the standard.

It wasn’t long after that when someone suggested, “Let’s go on a road trip.”

So on the next long weekend, six of us loaded into the car and headed toward Nelson. The car was big, spirits were high, and the sense of freedom was real.

We stopped in Kaikōura for fish and chips — the kind where being next to the ocean convinces you it’s the freshest fish you’ve ever eaten. Content and full, we headed off into the evening, driving toward Blenheim, where we were meeting friends before continuing on the next day.

Not far from Seddon, I heard a sound that makes any new car owner’s stomach drop.

A grinding noise.
Coming from one of the rear wheels.
Getting louder.

We pulled over, thinking something might have been caught up from the road.

Nothing.

We drove on a little further, but the noise persisted. Louder now. So I pulled over again — this time in front of a couple of isolated houses near the roadside.

We pushed on the wheel. Scratched our heads. Looked thoughtful without having much to contribute.

That’s when the owners of the house came out. One of the men, beer in hand, asked what was going on. I explained there was a noise coming from the rear wheel.

He turned to his mate, also holding a beer, as if consulting an oracle.

His mate looked at me and asked a simple question.

“Do you have any tools?”

My chest filled with pride.

“Yes,” I said. “I’ve got tools.”

It turned out he was on holiday — but he was also a mechanic.

He had no interest in getting his hands dirty, but he was more than happy to tell me what to do.

This was before cellphones, when breaking down meant relying on the public — and in New Zealand back then, people usually stopped and helped.

He wandered over to his friends garage, dragged out a beer crate, sat himself down comfortably, cracked open another beer, and proceeded to talk me through the repair step by step like it was the most normal thing in the world.

After a quick look and a few questions, he said we had a wheel bearing going.

He explained that if we packed it with grease, it would last the trip. When we got home, it would need to be replaced properly.

Relief washed over me.

For the first time, I got to use the tools I’d bought — not because I knew how, but because someone who did was standing nearby. He talked me through the steps while his friend fetched a tube of grease from the garage.

I followed instructions carefully. Packed the bearing. Put the wheel back on.

The mechanic nodded, confident.

Back on the road, the noise was gone.

And he was right.

We had an amazing trip.

Looking back, I still smile at that moment. I didn’t have the skill. I didn’t have the knowledge. But I had the tools — and sometimes that’s enough to bridge the gap until the right person shows up.

You never know who you’re going to meet.
