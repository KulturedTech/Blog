The Meditation Skeptic

When pain doesn’t behave like damage — and attention becomes access.

I had a back injury while at work that caused a slipped disc. It was a special kind of pain — the kind that could turn walking without a care into becoming a garden statue, going nowhere. One moment you’re fine. The next, you’re calculating every step like it might betray you. I did what you’re supposed to do. I followed the medical advice. I followed the specialists. I followed the physios. Scans. Exercises. Strengthening. Adjustments. Weeks turned into months. Months into years. There was improvement — just never freedom. At my next appointment, I told the doctor plainly that I was still struggling. He paused for a moment, then said something I wasn’t prepared for. “It might be in your head.” It landed badly. Not because I thought he was careless — but because it sounded like surrender dressed up as insight. I could feel exactly where the pain was. It had a location. A personality. A schedule. How could that be “in my head”? And even if it was — what was I supposed to do with that information? I wasn’t anxious. I wasn’t imagining things. I wasn’t stressed in any obvious way. I was injured. The suggestion didn’t feel helpful. It felt like the problem had been quietly handed back to me, with no instructions attached. ⸻ At the time, meditation wasn’t on my list of options. Not because I was hostile to it — but because it lived in a different world to painkillers, MRI scans, and exercise plans. It felt vague. Slightly indulgent. Not mechanical enough to be useful. Stillness didn’t sound like treatment. Awareness didn’t sound like repair. I needed something that did something. And yet, the idea wouldn’t leave. Not “maybe the pain is imaginary” — but maybe I don’t understand where pain lives. That question stayed. ⸻ I didn’t start meditating because I believed in it. I started because I had run out of explanations. At first, it wasn’t even “meditation.” It was just sitting still for a few minutes longer than felt reasonable, noticing how often my body braced itself before I asked it to move. I wasn’t trying to relax. I was trying to locate something. What surprised me wasn’t relief — it was patterns. The pain didn’t behave like a damaged part. It behaved like a guard. Certain movements triggered it before they happened. Certain thoughts tightened things before my body had even moved. I began to notice that pain wasn’t only reporting injury — it was anticipating threat. That idea would have sounded ridiculous to me earlier. Now it felt obvious. ⸻ I kept going, not out of discipline, but because something kept revealing itself. The more I noticed, the less I interfered. The less I interfered, the more space appeared. Some days nothing changed. Some days the pain softened for no reason I could measure. That was new. Nothing in my training had prepared me for improvement without effort. ⸻ Over time, the line between “physical” and “mental” stopped making sense. The body responded before I told it to. Breath affected tension. Attention affected pain. Not magically. Mechanically. Like discovering a light switch wired somewhere you’d never thought to look. ⸻ I didn’t abandon physio. I didn’t reject medicine. I didn’t replace anything. Meditation didn’t become a cure. It became access. Access to a system I’d been living in without a manual. ⸻ That’s when my skepticism shifted. Not into belief — but into something quieter. Respect.
