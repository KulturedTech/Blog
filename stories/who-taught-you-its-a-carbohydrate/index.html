Who Taught You It’s a Carbohydrate?

A reflection on how we learned to label food — before we ever learned to taste it.

We had just finished playing a game of touch rugby one evening and I had a banana in my bag that I started to eat. My friend said, “Ooh, be careful — you don’t want too many carbs this late. You’ll get fat.” I stopped for a moment and thought — From a banana? I didn’t challenge him on his statement. But then he pulled out an electrolyte replacement drink, coloured blue, and started drinking. That was it. That was the crack. The comment stayed with me longer than the game did. I found myself wondering how I already seemed to know which foods were carbohydrates. I hadn’t looked it up. I hadn’t studied it. I just knew. Who had told me? And why were there so many differences? Later, walking through a supermarket, I started noticing labels on food packets: Low Carb, Carb Free. I realised that’s where I’d learned it. Not from experience — from packaging. Flipping over product after product, there it was every time. The food list on the back, with carbohydrates singled out and given a number, as if it were the most important thing to know. We grew up memorising food pyramids the way kids now memorise app settings. Bright colours. Authoritative arrows. Very confident triangles. And just like most settings, no one remembers choosing them — only obeying them. ⸻ At some point in your life, someone pointed at a food and said, “That’s a carbohydrate.” Not with malice. Not with evidence. Just with confidence. And somehow… that was enough. You didn’t ask who decided that. You didn’t ask when. You definitely didn’t ask why a sweet potato and a jelly bean ended up in the same prison yard. You just nodded. Because adults were speaking. From that moment on, food stopped being food. It became a category. A number. A problem. ⸻ I watch people eat now the way I watch people speak a language they didn’t choose. They say things like: • “I can’t have carbs.” • “That’s bad after 6pm.” • “I was good today.” Good. As if lunch is a moral exam. Nobody says: • “This grew in soil.” • “This was once alive.” • “This feeds me.” They say “carbs” the way medieval villagers said “witch.” ⸻ Here’s the quiet trick nobody mentions: A carbohydrate is not a thing. It’s a label — invented so we could measure, control, and argue. It’s chemistry pretending to be wisdom. Rice doesn’t know it’s a carbohydrate. Potatoes didn’t attend the meeting. Fruit certainly didn’t sign the waiver. But one day, someone drew a line on a chart… …and now millions of people negotiate with their dinner like it owes them money. ⸻ I didn’t unlearn this in a book. I unlearned it standing at a stove. Heating a pan. Adding oil. Watching ginger hit steel and release something ancient. No numbers. No guilt. Just senses waking up. My body didn’t ask, “How many grams is this?” It asked, “Is this real?” ⸻ That’s when the question changed. Not: “What category is this?” But: “Who taught me to fear it?” Because fear always has a teacher. And it’s rarely the body. ⸻ Somewhere along the way, we stopped trusting appetite and started trusting spreadsheets. We outsourced wisdom to labels, then wondered why nothing tasted right anymore. We confused precision with truth. ⸻ So now, when someone tells me, “That’s a carbohydrate,” I smile. Not because they’re wrong — but because they’ve told me who they learned to listen to. And it probably wasn’t themselves. ⸻ Food is older than language. Older than nutrition science. Older than shame. It fed people long before it needed defending. So maybe the better question isn’t, “Is this a carbohydrate?” Maybe it’s: “Who taught you that was the most important thing to know?” And once you ask that… you don’t eat the same again.
